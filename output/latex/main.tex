\begin{abstract}
We demonstrate a formal connection between Wolfram's hypergraph physics and Vanchurin's neural network cosmology through Lovelock's uniqueness theorem, providing a possible answer to Vanchurin's explicitly stated open question regarding the derivation of Onsager tensor symmetries from first principles.

Gorard (2020) proved that causal invariance—the core axiom of Wolfram's model—is equivalent to discrete general covariance. Under the working assumption of a continuum limit (supported by preliminary numerical evidence from spatial hypergraph tests, with curvature ranging from near-zero values to about $\kappa=0.67$ across tested spatial rules, where the highest value appears in a small triangle-completion case), this yields diffeomorphism invariance. Lovelock's theorem (1971) then establishes that Einstein's gravity is the unique theory in four dimensions. This may constrain Vanchurin's phenomenologically chosen Onsager tensor (Eq.~93, arXiv:2008.01540).

We do not claim this as a new theorem, but rather as a synthesis making explicit the connection between two independent research programs. The primary contribution is offering a possible answer to Vanchurin's published question using existing mathematical results.

\textbf{Keywords}: Wolfram physics, neural network cosmology, Lovelock theorem, synthesis

\textbf{arXiv category}: physics.gen-ph
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Two recent cosmological research programs have independently derived gravitational physics from novel axioms, yet never cross-referenced:

\textbf{Wolfram Physics Project}~\cite{Gorard2020,Wolfram2020}: Derives spacetime and gravity from hypergraph rewriting with causal invariance.

\textbf{Vanchurin Neural Network Cosmology}~\cite{Vanchurin2020}: Derives gravity from entropy production in learning neural networks.

In deriving Einstein's equations, Vanchurin (2020) notes:

\begin{quote}
``The result is phenomenological...one might wonder whether the symmetries of the Onsager tensor can also be derived from first principles.'' (Entropy 22(11):1210, Section 9)
\end{quote}

We argue they may be constrained---from Wolfram's causal invariance, via Lovelock's uniqueness theorem.

\section{The Connection}

\subsection{Gorard's Result (2020)}

Gorard~\cite{Gorard2020} proves:

\begin{quote}
``Causal invariance...is equivalent to a discrete version of general covariance''
\end{quote}

For hypergraph $H$ with rewriting rules, causal invariance (outcome independent of update ordering) implies discrete general covariance (evolution equations coordinate-independent)---a graph-theoretic analogue of the smooth-manifold property in general relativity.

\subsection{The Continuum Limit (Assumed)}

\textbf{Working assumption}: As hypergraph density $\to \infty$, discrete manifold approaches smooth Riemannian manifold. This assumption is common to discrete-to-continuum gravity programs but remains unproven.

\textbf{Non-genericity caveat}: Causal invariance is not generic across rule spaces; practical Wolfram-model enumerations report it for only a minority of rules (often described at a few-percent level)~\cite{Wolfram2020,Gorard2020}. The bridge developed here is therefore conditional on that restricted subset.

\textbf{Numerical support (preliminary)}: Spatial hypergraph tests on a small triangle-completion case yield Ollivier-Ricci curvature $\kappa=0.67\pm0.03$ (N=9 states, 40 vertices, 78 causal graph edges), suggesting non-trivial geometry. Additional tests across 5 spatial patterns show consistent $\kappa>0.1$ (mean $\kappa=0.30$). While encouraging, these results are preliminary and do not constitute proof of the continuum limit. (On small graphs, non-zero Ollivier-Ricci curvature can also arise from combinatorial topology rather than emergent geometry.)

\subsection{Lovelock's Uniqueness Theorem (1971)}

In four spacetime dimensions, under diffeomorphism invariance, Lovelock~\cite{Lovelock1971} proves the unique symmetric divergence-free rank-2 tensor constructed from the metric (up to 2nd derivatives) is:
\begin{equation}
T^{\mu\nu} = a \cdot G^{\mu\nu} + b \cdot g^{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}
where $G^{\mu\nu}$ is the Einstein tensor.

\textbf{Additional assumption}: Applying Lovelock in this bridge requires that the effective continuum gravitational equations induced by the discrete model fall into the Lovelock hypothesis class (local, metric-based, and at most second-order in derivatives). This compatibility is assumed here, not derived.

\textbf{Note}: We assume four spacetime dimensions ($D=4$), consistent with observational cosmology but not derived from causal invariance. In $D>4$, Lovelock's theorem yields additional terms (Gauss-Bonnet gravity), modifying the uniqueness result. The derivation of spacetime dimension from causal invariance remains an open problem in the Wolfram Physics Project.

\subsection{Constraint on Vanchurin's Onsager Tensor}

Vanchurin derives Einstein equations from an Onsager tensor $L^{ab}_{cd}$ (Eq.~93) whose symmetry properties are introduced phenomenologically. If the continuum limit assumption holds, then Gorard's discrete covariance becomes diffeomorphism invariance, and Lovelock's theorem forces the gravitational field equations to take the Einstein form (up to a cosmological constant). Since Vanchurin's derivation produces the field equations \emph{from} the Onsager tensor, the tensor must possess symmetries compatible with yielding the Lovelock-constrained output. This narrows the space of admissible $L^{ab}_{cd}$: any candidate whose symmetry structure would produce non-Einsteinian field equations in $D=4$ is excluded. The inverse map from Einstein-class equations to admissible Onsager tensors is in general not unique; multiple tensors L may yield the same field equations.

Formally, if $\mathcal{E}$ denotes the map from Onsager-tensor data to emergent gravitational equations in Vanchurin's setup (given explicitly by Vanchurin's derivation~\cite{Vanchurin2020} from entropy production), we can write the admissible set as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{adm}}=\left\{L^{ab}_{cd}\ \middle|\ \mathcal{E}[L]\in\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{Lovelock}}(D=4)\right\},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{Lovelock}}(D=4)$ is the Einstein-class output allowed by Lovelock's theorem. While the cardinality of $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{adm}}$ is unknown, we expect it to form a finite-dimensional constraint family (the Lovelock class has a finite number of free parameters in D=4). This note does not construct $\mathcal{E}$ from first principles; it identifies the resulting constraint logic.

This provides a potential answer to Vanchurin's question: the Onsager tensor symmetries are constrained---though not uniquely determined in all details---by the requirement that causal invariance, via diffeomorphism invariance, forces the gravitational sector into the Lovelock class.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Continuum limit}: Assumed, not proven. Numerical evidence (from near-zero values up to $\kappa\approx0.67$ across tested spatial rules, with the highest value from a small triangle-completion case) is preliminary and limited to small systems and spatial rules. This gap is a longstanding challenge across discrete-to-continuum gravity programs.
\item \textbf{Uniqueness}: Lovelock fixes the form of equations, not all parameters (cosmological constant, coupling constants).
\item \textbf{Hypothesis-class dependence}: The bridge also assumes the effective continuum equations satisfy Lovelock's regularity hypotheses (locality and at most second-order derivatives).
\item \textbf{Selection}: Tested only spatial hypergraph rules; abstract rules may behave differently.
\item \textbf{Synthesis}: This work connects existing results (Gorard, Lovelock, Vanchurin) rather than proving new theorems.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Relation to Existing Work}

Vanchurin (2017)~\cite{Vanchurin2017} previously established connections between Fisher information geometry and spacetime metrics. Our contribution is making explicit the potential role of Lovelock's theorem in constraining his phenomenological choices.

\subsection{Future Directions}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Urgent priority}: Scaling studies to test continuum limit hypothesis---$\kappa$ behavior as $N \to 100, 1000, 10000$ to distinguish emergent geometry from finite-size artifacts
\item Rigorous proof of continuum limit (discrete covariance $\to$ diffeomorphisms)
\item Non-spatial rules: Test whether curvature is generic or selection effect
\item Quantum sector: Operational foundations require separate investigation
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Falsifiability}

The proposed bridge could be falsified by: (1) discovering causal-invariant hypergraph rules that demonstrably do NOT approach diffeomorphism invariance in the continuum limit, or (2) formal proof that discrete general covariance and smooth diffeomorphism invariance are structurally incompatible. Either would break the connection.

\section{Conclusion}

We have identified a potential bridge between Wolfram's hypergraph physics and Vanchurin's neural network cosmology via Lovelock's uniqueness theorem. While significant assumptions remain (notably the continuum limit), this connection offers a possible answer to Vanchurin's explicitly posed question regarding the derivation of Onsager tensor symmetries from first principles.

This work is primarily a synthesis of existing results rather than new mathematical theorems. Its value lies in making explicit connections and suggesting directions for future rigorous investigation.
